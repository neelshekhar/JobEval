Foundations
What is Job Evaluation?

Job Evaluation (JE) is a structured, systematic process for assessing the relative size of roles within an organisation. Unlike a performance review — which assesses how well a person is doing — JE assesses the job itself: its scope, complexity, accountability, and required expertise, regardless of who holds it.

The output is a score (or grade) that positions the role on a scale, making it possible to compare roles across functions, geographies, and even companies. This creates a consistent foundation for decisions about compensation, career architecture, and levelling.

Key distinction
JE evaluates the role, not the person in it. Two people at the same role get the same JE score — regardless of performance or tenure. A promotion means the role's scope has permanently shifted, not just that the person has worked hard.

Methodologies
Common JE approaches

There are four widely used JE methodologies. Most large enterprises use a point-factor approach because it is transparent, scalable, and linkable to global compensation databases.

Point-Factor
Most common

Roles are scored on multiple factors (e.g. scope, complexity, accountability). Scores are summed into a total point value. Mercer IPE and Hay Group use this approach.

Job Ranking
Simple

Roles are ranked from largest to smallest based on overall perceived value. Fast but highly subjective — hard to defend at scale.

Job Classification
Grade-based

Predefined grade descriptions are created, and jobs are slotted into the closest matching grade. Common in government and public sector.

Factor Comparison
Hybrid

A hybrid of ranking and point-factor. Benchmark jobs are ranked on individual factors, then used to score other roles. Technically rigorous but rarely used today.

Mercer IPE
International Position Evaluation — how it works

Mercer's IPE is the most widely adopted JE methodology globally, used by 20,000+ organisations across 150+ countries. It is a point-factor system that evaluates every role on five dimensions. The weighted sum of scores across these dimensions becomes the IPE score, which maps to a global compensation database covering millions of incumbents.

Why IPE?
Because IPE is global and standardised, Swiggy's internal grades can be directly mapped to Mercer's annual remuneration survey — enabling true market benchmarking. A B9 at Swiggy is genuinely comparable to a similar-scored role at any other participating company.

Promotion context
Why JE matters for promotion decisions

JE scores make promotion conversations more objective. A promotion isn't just a title change — it's a claim that the role's scope, complexity, and accountability have permanently shifted upward across most dimensions.

If your IPE score lands in the next band, it validates that the role has genuinely grown, not just that the person has worked hard in their current one. This is why calibration committees look at IPE scores alongside performance: they are asking whether the seat has grown, not just the person sitting in it.

Practical implication
Use the IPE Scorecard tab to score your current role. If most of your factor scores already sit in the next band, that's a strong signal your scope has grown and a promotion conversation is warranted. If only one or two factors are elevated, the role hasn't fully shifted — keep building the others.
